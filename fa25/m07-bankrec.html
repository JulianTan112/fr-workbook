Why deposits-in-transit and unpresented cheques stay on the bank side: the business has already recorded them in the Cash at Bank ledger. The bank has not processed them yet, so no general journal entry is passed.
Why EFT, dishonoured cheque, bank charges and book error go to the books side: these items are missing from or wrong in the business ledger. They must be journalised before the ledger balance can agree with the adjusted bank balance.

Why unpresented cheques are deducted from the bank statement balance

Core logic: an unpresented cheque has already been recorded by the business as a cash payment, so the company ledger already treats the money as gone. The bank statement is temporarily too high because the payee has not deposited or cashed the cheque yet.

PointMeaning
Timing differenceThe business has written and recorded the cheque, so Cash at Bank in the books has already decreased.
Bank delayThe bank does not know about the cheque until the payee presents it for payment.
Reconciliation aimDeduct the unpresented cheque from the bank statement balance so the bank side matches the realistic, up-to-date cash balance.
Simple example:
Bank statement balance before presentation        $1,000
Less: cheque written but not yet presented          (200)
Adjusted bank-side cash balance                     $800

Books already show: $1,000 − $200 = $800

Sandoz application: cheque 436 ($1,012) and cheque 455 ($5,300) are deducted from the bank side because Sandoz has already recorded these payments, but the bank has not yet processed them. The deduction prevents cash from being overstated.

Bank statement balance                            $10,109
Add: 31 Oct cash sales not yet banked               4,339
Subtotal                                           14,448
Less: unpresented cheques ($1,012 + $5,300)        (6,312)
Adjusted cash balance                              $8,136

If you do not deduct them: available cash is overstated, future payments may bounce, and the cash balance in the financial statements will be inaccurate.

Self-check before moving on: make sure every debit has a matching credit, statement effects are separated from cash effects, and timing items are not accidentally journalised.

Explanation for Sandoz reconciliation, journals and petty cash control

Part (a) bank reconciliation bridge

Bank side:
$10,109 + $4,339 − $1,012 − $5,300 = $8,136

Cash book side:
$9,641 + $850 − $2,220 − $35 − $100 = $8,136

The two sides reconcile to the same adjusted cash balance. Cheque 440 and the $5,529 deposit are not adjusted again because they already appear correctly in the records being reconciled.

Part (b) journal-entry bridge

EFT receipt:             Dr Cash 850 / Cr Accounts Receivable 850
Dishonoured cheque:      Dr Accounts Receivable 2,220 / Cr Cash 2,220
Bank charges:            Dr Bank charges 35 / Cr Cash 35
Ole receipt error:       Dr Accounts Receivable 100 / Cr Cash 100

Cash ledger check:
+$850 − $2,220 − $35 − $100 = −$1,505
$9,641 − $1,505 = $8,136

Journalise only the book-side items because these are missing from or wrong in the business records. Bank-side timing items explain the bank statement difference but do not need business journal entries.

Part (c) internal-control bridge

Explain each control point as weakness → risk → control. Returning original receipts and keeping only a petty-cash-book entry creates weak evidence and duplicate-claim risk. Stronger control uses pre-numbered vouchers, attached receipts, “PAID” stamps, approval signatures and independent review.

Explanation for part (b): why these four journal entries are made

EFT receipt — $850: the bank statement shows money received from Core Life, but the cash book has not recorded it. So Cash at Bank increases and Core Life's receivable is cleared: Dr Cash at Bank $850; Cr Accounts Receivable — Core Life $850.

Dishonoured cheque — $2,220: Kool Work's cheque was previously treated as collected, but the bank rejected it. The business must reverse the cash receipt and reinstate the customer debt: Dr Accounts Receivable — Kool Work $2,220; Cr Cash at Bank $2,220.

Bank charges — $35: the bank has already deducted the charge, but the business has not recorded the expense. So record the expense and reduce cash: Dr Bank Charges Expense $35; Cr Cash at Bank $35.

Ole receipt error — $100: the cash book recorded Ole's receipt as $5,566, but the bank shows only $5,466. Cash is overstated by $100, and Ole still owes that amount. So Dr Accounts Receivable — Ole $100; Cr Cash at Bank $100.

Part (b) cash ledger check: net cash adjustment is +$850 − $2,220 − $35 − $100 = −$1,505. Cash at Bank therefore moves from $9,641 to $8,136.

(c) Internal control weaknesses — petty cash

Internal control answer format

Petty cash weaknesses and improvements

Weakness identifiedRisk createdRecommended control
Original receipt is returned to the employee after reimbursement.The same receipt may be reused for another claim. The business also loses its audit evidence.Attach the original receipt to a pre-numbered petty cash voucher and stamp it PAID.
The only evidence retained is an entry in the petty cash book.The entry is not enough to prove the claim was genuine, authorised, and business-related.Maintain a petty cash voucher file with receipt, claimant signature, approval signature, date, and expense code.
The process does not show independent approval before payment.Personal or fictitious claims may be reimbursed.Require approval by a responsible officer before reimbursement, separate from the petty cash custodian.
Custody, recording, and checking are not clearly separated.The same person may hide errors or misuse cash.Perform periodic surprise cash counts and reconcile cash on hand plus vouchers to the imprest amount.

Explanation for part (c): how to answer the petty cash control requirement

This part is not asking for journal entries. It asks for weaknesses and improvements. A strong answer must separate three things: weakness identified → risk created → recommended control.

Weakness 1: returning the original receipt to the employee is unsafe because the same receipt can be reused for another claim. The control is to attach the original receipt to a pre-numbered petty cash voucher and stamp it PAID.

Weakness 2: keeping only the petty cash book entry is weak evidence because it does not prove the claim was genuine, authorised, and business-related. The control is to keep a voucher file with receipt, claimant signature, approval signature, date and expense code.

Extra acceptable points: lack of independent approval creates risk of personal or fictitious claims; poor segregation of custody, recording and checking creates risk that errors or misuse can be hidden.

Part (c) self-check: do not write only “improve internal control”. Each markable point should name the weakness, explain the risk, and give a matching control.
