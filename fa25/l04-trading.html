🛒 Accounting for Trading Businesses

Merchandising accounting: inventory purchases, sales, returns, freight, discounts, shortages and trading statements.

Learning outcomes

  • Differentiate service and trading businesses.
  • Prepare trading-business SPL sections: sales, Cost of goods sold, gross profit, operating expenses and net profit.
  • Record purchases and sales under the perpetual inventory system.
  • Record returns, allowances, transportation costs, discounts and inventory shortage or surplus.
  • Apply goods-in-transit ownership cut-off using FCA and CPT terms.

Why It Matters — learning outcome bridge

These points explain why the learning outcomes are not isolated textbook headings. They show what the learner should be able to do in a practice or exam setting.

Sales are not gross profit

Merchandising questions require two layers: selling price to revenue, inventory cost to Cost of goods sold.

Cost of sales drives margin

A small error in Cost of goods sold affects gross profit, operating profit, inventory and equity.

Cut-off protects ownership accuracy

FCA or CPT, consignment and goods-in-transit decide whether inventory belongs in the SFP.

Additional Insights — source-reinforced

Reinforced from the Financial Accounting and Financial Management reference attachments. This is not extra memorisation; it shows how the lesson connects to decision-usefulness, statement analysis and exam interpretation.

Perpetual system

Record inventory movement continuously: sale entry plus cost entry.

Gross profit analysis

Gross profit = Sales revenue − Cost of sales. Gross profit margin explains how much of each sales dollar remains after inventory cost.

Operating cost separation

Freight-in is inventory cost for buyer; freight-out or delivery is selling expense for seller.

Applied explanation bridge — trading activity to gross profit

A trading business earns profit by buying goods for resale and selling them to customers. The accounting answer must separate the revenue side from the inventory-cost side.

Source factJournal or schedule logicSPL effectSFP effect
Goods purchased for resale.Record Inventory under the perpetual system unless facts say otherwise.No immediate expense until goods are sold or written down.Current asset increases.
Goods sold to customer.Record sales revenue and remove the inventory cost through cost of goods sold.Sales increases revenue; cost of goods sold reduces gross profit.Cash or receivables increase; inventory decreases.
Sales returns, allowances and discounts.Reduce gross sales to net sales.Net sales is used before deriving gross profit.Receivables or cash may decrease depending on the case.
Freight terms such as FCA or CPT.Use the shipping term to decide ownership at year-end.Only goods owned by the buyer affect inventory and cost flows.Inventory in transit may be recognised if ownership has transferred.
Terminology guardrail: “Cost of sales” is the SPL presentation line. “Cost of goods sold” is the inventory-cost working used in trading questions and inventory turnover calculations. Do not merge them lazily.

Formula focus — source-based formulas

The formula is separated from the explanation. Copy the formula route first, then substitute numbers from the correct statement.

L04 or L06

Gross profit

FormulaSales revenue − Cost of sales
Trace statement inputs:

Sales revenue − cost of sales = gross profit.

How to read it:

Use after adjusting purchases, returns, freight-in, closing inventory, shortage or surplus and obsolescence.

L04, L06 or L10

Gross profit margin

FormulaGross profit / Sales revenue × 100
Trace statement inputs:

Gross profit divided by sales revenue. Gross profit is sales revenue less cost of sales.

How to read it:

Shows how much sales remains after inventory cost. Do not deduct admin, selling or finance costs here.

1. Service vs trading businesses

Service businesses earn revenue by providing services. Trading or merchandising businesses earn profit by buying goods for resale and selling them to customers. The major difference in performance reporting is that trading businesses show cost of goods sold and gross profit.

Profit computation comparison
Business typeRevenue lineCost structureProfit presentation
Service businessService revenueOperating expensesService revenue − operating expenses = net profit.
Trading businessSales revenueCost of goods sold plus operating expensesSales − Cost of goods sold = gross profit; gross profit − operating expenses = net profit.

3. Purchases and sales under perpetual inventory

Source facts before the perpetual inventory purchase and sale entries:
Case factAccounting treatment
Goods purchased for $5,000 cash.Debit Inventory because goods for resale are acquired; credit Cash.
Goods purchased for $5,000 on credit from Supplier S.Debit Inventory; credit Accounts Payable.
Goods sold to Customer B on credit for selling price $5,000.Debit Accounts Receivable; credit Sales.
The inventory sold cost the business $3,500.Debit Cost of goods sold; credit Inventory to remove the goods at cost.
Perpetual sale needs two figures from the case: selling price 5,000 for revenue, and inventory cost 3,500 for cost transfer.

Boundary check: The selling price and inventory cost are different facts. Do not use the sales amount as the inventory cost unless the question states it.

4. Returns and allowances

Returns and allowances
EventBuyer or seller actionJournal patternInventory impact
Purchase returnBuyer returns goods to supplier before payment.Dr Accounts Payable; Cr Inventory.Inventory decreases because the buyer no longer holds those goods.
Purchase allowanceSupplier gives price reduction for defective or unacceptable goods kept by buyer.Dr Accounts Payable; Cr Inventory.Inventory cost is reduced.
Sales return — goods in good conditionCustomer returns goods and seller accepts return.Dr Sales Returns; Cr Accounts Receivable or Cash. Also Dr Inventory; Cr Cost of goods sold.Inventory is restored at cost.

5. Transportation costs and goods-in-transit cut-off

Freight terms determine when ownership and risk transfer, which determines whether goods in transit are included in the buyer’s inventory at year-end.

Freight and ownership cut-off
Term used in courseWhen buyer includes goodsTypical treatment
FCA named place or origin-style transferWhen seller delivers goods to carrier at the named place.Buyer owns goods while in transit; freight-in may be included in inventory cost if necessary to bring inventory to sale location.
CPT named destination or destination-style transferWhen goods arrive at destination.Buyer excludes goods before arrival; seller retains inventory until delivery point.
Source facts before the goods-in-transit FCA example:
Case factAccounting treatment
Goods were shipped under FCA Bali before year-end.Buyer has taken ownership once the seller delivers goods to the carrier at the named place.
Supplier invoice for the goods is $1,200.Credit Accounts Payable to the supplier.
Freight or shipping cost is $300.Include as part of inventory cost when it is necessary to bring inventory to sale location.
Goods are still in transit at reporting date.Physical location does not override ownership transfer.
Inventory recognised = supplier invoice 1,200 + freight-in 300 = 1,500

8. Trading-business statement presentation

Trading-business SPL extract
LineComputation
Sales revenueGross sales less sales returns and allowances and sales discounts if applicable.
Cost of goods soldInventory cost transferred to expense when goods are sold, adjusted for shortages or surpluses.
Gross profitNet sales − Cost of goods sold.
Operating expensesSelling and administrative expenses, such as freight-out if seller pays delivery to customer.
Net profitGross profit − operating expenses.
