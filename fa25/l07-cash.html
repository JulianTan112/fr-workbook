Why It Matters — learning outcome bridge

These points explain why the learning outcomes are not isolated textbook headings. They show what the learner should be able to do in a practice or exam setting.

Cash is high-risk

Cash is liquid, easy to conceal and therefore needs strong segregation and authorisation controls.

Bank reconciliation separates bank timing from book errors

Reconciling items explain bank balance; adjusting items require journal entries in the books.

Receivables are not automatically collectible

Allowance accounting reports receivables at estimated collectible value.

Formula focus — source-based formulas

The formula is separated from the explanation. Copy the formula route first, then substitute numbers from the correct statement.

L07 or L10

Acid-test or quick ratio

Formula(Current assets − Inventory) / Current liabilities
Trace statement inputs:

Quick assets divided by current liabilities. Quick assets exclude inventory.

How to read it:

Stricter liquidity test because inventory may not turn quickly into cash.

L07

Receivables turnover

FormulaCredit sales / Average trade receivables
Trace source:

Use credit sales from sales records or SPL sales if all sales are credit, and average trade receivables from SFP trade receivables.

How to read it:

Measures how often receivables are converted into cash during the year.

L07

Receivables collection period

FormulaAverage trade receivables / Credit sales × 365
Trace source:

Use average trade receivables from SFP trade receivables and credit sales from sales records or SPL sales if all sales are credit.

How to read it:

Measures average days customers take to pay.

L07 or L10

Payables settlement period

FormulaAverage trade payables / Credit purchases × 365
Trace source:

Use average trade payables from the SFP and credit purchases from purchase records. If credit purchases are not given, use the amount specified by the question.

How to read it:

Measures average days taken to pay suppliers.

L07 or L10

Operating cash cycle

FormulaInventory days + Receivables days − Payables days
Trace source:

This combines three separate working-capital timing measures: inventory days + receivable days − payable days. The components are built from SPL or SFP inputs and credit records.

How to read it:

Shows how long cash is tied up in inventory and receivables before supplier credit offsets it.

2. Internal control over cash

Cash is highly liquid, portable and vulnerable because it can be hidden, moved and used without leaving the same trail as electronic records. Controls protect cash from receipt to banking and ensure disbursements are authorised business payments.

Cash controls
Control areaControlsPurpose
Cash receiptsSegregate cash handling from recording; bank cash intact; bank daily; keep cash under lock and key.Prevent theft and ensure complete recording.
Cash disbursementsUse cheques or EFT where possible; require authorisation; match payment to invoice or voucher; authorised signatory.Prevent fictitious or unauthorised payments.
Independent checksReconcile records, surprise counts, supervisory review.Detect errors and fraud.

3. Petty cash system

Petty cash is a fund for small day-to-day payments such as postage, stationery and transport claims. The system should use a custodian, petty cash vouchers, approval, verification, acknowledgement and receipt attachment.

Source facts before the petty cash journal entries:
Case factAccounting treatment
Petty cash fund is established at $500.Debit Petty Cash and credit Cash at Bank for $500.
Petty cash vouchers or expenses total $350 at replenishment date.Debit the relevant expense accounts and credit Cash at Bank for $350.
The imprest fund returns to $500 after replenishment.Do not credit Petty Cash during normal replenishment; only adjust Petty Cash when the fund size changes.
Cash at bank paid on replenishment = vouchers reimbursed = 350. Petty Cash account remains at 500 unless the float is increased or decreased.

4. Bank reconciliation

A bank reconciliation explains the difference between the bank statement balance and the business Cash at Bank ledger. Bank-side items are timing differences or bank errors. Book-side items require journal entries because the business records are incomplete or wrong.

Bank reconciliation classification
ItemBank side or Books side?Why
Deposits-in-transitBank sideRecorded by business, not yet shown by bank.
Unpresented chequesBank sideRecorded by business, not yet cleared by bank.
Bank errorsBank sideBank’s records need correction; business records are assumed correct if question says so.
EFT receipt or direct creditBooks sideBank has recorded cash receipt; business must record it.
Bank chargesBooks sideBank deducted fee; business must record expense.
Dishonoured chequeBooks sideCash receipt reversed by bank; customer still owes business.
Business recording errorBooks sideBusiness ledger must be corrected.

7. Impairment and contra accounts in receivables

Impairment means expected loss in asset recoverability. For receivables, the weak point is separating the customer's gross debt from the amount expected to be collected.

Receivables impairment logic
AccountTypeNormal balancePurpose
Accounts ReceivableAssetDebitGross amount owed by customers.
Allowance for Impairment of Accounts ReceivableContra-assetCreditEstimated portion of receivables not expected to be collected.
Impairment of Accounts Receivable or Bad Debt ExpenseExpenseDebitLoss recognised in SPL when expected uncollectible debts are estimated or directly written off.
Writeback of ImpairmentIncome or expense reversalCreditUsed when a previous impairment is reduced or recovered in a later period, depending on the question convention.
Allowance account movement
EventDebitCreditEffect on net receivables
Estimate required allowance increasesImpairment of Accounts ReceivableAllowance for Impairment of Accounts ReceivableNet receivables decrease.

Exam trap: Under the allowance method, a write-off is not a new impairment expense. The expense was recognised earlier when the allowance was estimated.
