1. Business purpose, risk-return and shareholder involvement

Why businesses exist

From a financial-management lens, a business is expected to create value for owners while operating within risk, ethical and stakeholder constraints. Financial Accounting supports this by reporting performance, position and changes in equity.

Balancing risk and return

Higher expected returns usually come with higher risk. Accounting numbers do not remove risk, but they help users assess profit stability, debt burden, liquidity and asset quality.

Shareholder involvement

Shareholders use financial reports, votes, dividends and market information to monitor whether directors act in owners' interests.

Rewarding directors

Director remuneration is not just an expense classification issue. It also raises governance questions: are rewards aligned with company performance and shareholder interests?

2. Statement interpretation insights

Cost of sales / COGS

FA link
Recorded through trading/inventory entries.
Insight
Gross profit falls when cost of sales rises faster than sales revenue.
Formula
Gross profit = Sales revenue - Cost of sales.

Operating costs

FA link
Selling, administrative and financial expense classifications.
Insight
Operating profit can fall even when gross profit is stable if operating expenses are not controlled.
Warning
Do not mix finance cost with selling/admin operating expenses unless the question's layout says so.

Sources of cash inflows

Examples
Cash sales, collection from receivables, owner/share issue, loan proceeds, asset disposal.
FA link
Cash at Bank ledger and bank reconciliation.
Insight
Profit and cash can move differently because of credit sales, accruals, prepayments and depreciation.

Sources of cash outflows

Examples
Supplier payments, wages, expenses, loan interest, dividends, tax payment, PPE purchase.
FA link
Cash account, payable settlement, dividend payable and tax payable.
Insight
A profitable business can still struggle if cash is tied up in inventories and receivables.

Statement of financial position

FA link
Assets = Liabilities + Equity.
Insight
Assets show resources controlled; liabilities and equity show claims/financing.
Exam use
Use current/non-current classification to explain liquidity and financing structure.

Financing gap

Meaning
Extra finance needed when planned assets/outflows exceed available internal funding and existing finance.
FA link
Projected SFP or cash budget may reveal a shortfall.
Action lens
Could be covered by borrowing, shares, retained profit, better receivable collection, lower inventories or slower payable settlement.

3. Financial ratio bank for accounting learners

Ratios are interpretation tools. They do not replace journal entries or statement preparation, but they explain what the finished statements reveal.

Profitability ratios

RatioFormulaWhat it tells you
ROSFProfit available to ordinary shareholders / Average ordinary shareholders' funds × 100Return earned for ordinary shareholders.
ROCEProfit before interest and tax / Average capital employed × 100Return generated from long-term capital.
Operating profit marginOperating profit / Sales revenue × 100Operating profit left from each dollar of sales.
Gross profit marginGross profit / Sales revenue × 100Profit left after cost of sales before other expenses.

Efficiency ratios

RatioFormulaWhat it tells you
Average inventories turnover periodAverage inventories / Cost of sales × 365 daysHow long inventory is held before sale.
Average receivables settlement periodAverage trade receivables / Credit sales × 365 daysHow long customers take to pay.
Average payables settlement periodAverage trade payables / Credit purchases × 365 daysHow long business takes to pay suppliers.
Sales revenue to capital employedSales revenue / Capital employedHow efficiently capital generates sales.
Sales revenue per employeeSales revenue / Number of employeesSales productivity per employee.

Liquidity and gearing ratios

RatioFormulaWhat it tells you
Current ratioCurrent assets / Current liabilitiesShort-term asset cover for short-term obligations.
Acid test ratio(Current assets - inventories) / Current liabilitiesLiquid asset cover excluding inventory.
Gearing ratioLong-term borrowings / (Equity + long-term borrowings) × 100Reliance on debt financing. Formula variants exist; use the one given.
Interest coverProfit before interest and tax / Interest expenseAbility of profit to cover finance cost.

Investment ratios

RatioFormulaWhat it tells you
Dividend payout ratioOrdinary dividends / Earnings available for ordinary shareholders × 100Proportion of earnings distributed.
Dividend yieldDividend per share / Market price per share × 100Cash dividend return relative to share price.
Earnings per shareEarnings available to ordinary shareholders / Weighted average ordinary sharesEarnings attributable to each ordinary share.
Price/earnings ratioMarket price per share / Earnings per shareMarket price relative to current earnings.

4. Working capital and the operating cash cycle

Operating cash cycle: Cash → Inventory purchase/production → Sale to customer → Receivable collection → Cash again. Supplier credit can delay the cash outflow.

Working capital

Working capital is usually analysed as current assets less current liabilities. In this workbook, the main accounting pieces are inventories, trade receivables, cash, trade payables and overdrafts.

Overtrading warning

Fast sales growth can still create cash pressure when inventories and receivables grow faster than the business can finance them.

Cash discounts

Discounts for early settlement link Lesson 04 and Lesson 07: the accounting entry records the discount, while the management question asks whether early payment is worth the cash cost/benefit.

How much cash should be held?

Too little cash creates liquidity risk; too much idle cash may reduce returns. Financial Accounting records the cash balance; financial management judges whether the balance is efficient.

Managing trade payables

Payables can act like short-term supplier finance, but late payment may damage supplier relationships and credit terms.

Free cash flow lens

Free cash flow focuses on cash available after maintaining operations and investment needs. It is useful for interpretation, but it is not a normal journal-entry topic in this workbook.

5. Capital investment appraisal: useful context for PPE

These techniques are Financial Management tools. They explain how a business may decide whether to buy a major asset, but the Financial Accounting entries still follow PPE recognition, depreciation and disposal rules.

Accounting Rate of Return (ARR)

Uses accounting profit to compare return with the investment base.

ARR = Average annual accounting profit / Average investment × 100

Payback Period (PP)

Measures how long it takes for project cash inflows to recover the original outlay.

Payback = Time taken to recover initial investment from cash flows

Net Present Value (NPV)

Discounts future cash flows to today's value, then deducts the initial investment.

NPV = Present value of future cash flows - Initial investment

Discount tables and cost of capital

Discount tables convert future cash flows into present values. The discount rate often reflects the business's cost of capital and risk.

Internal Rate of Return (IRR)

The discount rate that makes NPV equal zero. It is a decision yield, not an accounting profit margin.

Risk-adjusted discount rate

Higher-risk projects may be assessed using a higher discount rate. This is a decision appraisal issue, not a PPE ledger entry.

6. Sources of finance and statement classification

Ordinary shares

Equity finance. In Financial Accounting, cash or assets received are credited to share capital according to the share issue treatment.

Preference shares

May be equity or liability depending on redemption obligations and terms. This links directly to Lesson 09 and Lesson 10 classification.

Loans and borrowings

Debt finance creates liabilities and interest expense. Non-current/current classification depends on timing of settlement.

Short-term external finance

Bank overdrafts, factoring and invoice discounting are ways to finance short-term cash needs. In accounting, identify whether the arrangement creates cash, liability, expense or receivable effects.

Retained profits

Internal long-term finance. Accounting shows it inside equity; financial management asks whether profits should be retained or paid as dividends.

Reducing inventory levels

Can release cash tied up in working capital. But inventory must still be sufficient for expected sales demand.

Effect of financial gearing

Borrowing can increase shareholder returns when profits are strong, but it also increases fixed finance-cost pressure and risk.

Dividend policy

Dividends distribute profits to shareholders. In accounting, declared dividends create dividend payable or reduce retained earnings; in finance, policy affects shareholder expectations and retained funding.

7. Advanced valuation and shareholder-value concepts

Economic Value Added (EVA)

Useful to understand performance after considering capital cost. Not needed for normal Financial Accounting journal entries.

Shareholder value approaches

Useful for business valuation and strategy. Treat as advanced decision context, not core FA treatment.

Terminal value and asset-based methods

Useful in valuation. The SFP method can use accounting net assets as a starting point, but accounting book value is not automatically business market value.
