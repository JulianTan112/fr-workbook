Learning outcome: Why It Matters summary

Lesson 01 · Regulatory Environment

  • Users decide with accounting, not vibes: Investors, lenders and creditors need information about resources, claims and changes in them before they provide capital.
  • The Conceptual Framework prevents random answers: Relevance, faithful representation, cost constraint, accrual basis and going concern explain why a treatment is acceptable.
  • Ethics protects usefulness: A biased estimate may still look neat in a statement, but it destroys neutrality and faithful representation.

Lesson 02 · Accounting Equation & Financial Statements

  • The equation is the skeleton of every answer: Assets = liabilities + equity explains why every transaction must keep the records balanced.
  • Statements are connected, not separate islands: Profit flows into equity; equity and balances appear in the SFP; cash movements explain liquidity.
  • Business structure changes presentation: Sole proprietorship, partnership and company forms affect capital, drawings/dividends and equity labels.

Lesson 03 · Recording Business Transactions

  • The cycle turns evidence into statements: Source documents are analysed, journalised, posted to ledger, checked by trial balance and summarised in statements.
  • Debit/credit is a language system: AED/LRC prevents guessing and lets learners build entries from account nature.
  • Trial balance is only an arithmetic check: It proves total debits equal total credits, not that every treatment is correct.

Lesson 04 · Trading Businesses

  • Sales are not gross profit: Merchandising questions require two layers: selling price to revenue, inventory cost to COGS.
  • Cost of sales drives margin: A small error in COGS affects gross profit, operating profit, inventory and equity.
  • Cut-off protects ownership accuracy: FCA/CPT, consignment and goods-in-transit decide whether inventory belongs in the SFP.

Lesson 05 · Balance Day Adjustments

  • Accrual accounting gives period truth: Revenue and expenses belong to the period earned/incurred, not simply when cash moves.
  • Adjustments repair timing: Prepaids, accruals, unearned revenue and depreciation update accounts before statements.
  • Cash flow and profit differ: Cash receipts/payments are important, but accrual profit better depicts performance for the period.

Lesson 06 · Inventories

  • Inventory affects both SFP and SPL: Ending inventory changes assets; COGS changes gross profit and equity.
  • Ownership beats physical location: Goods can be in transit or held by another party but still belong to the reporting entity.
  • NRV stops overstated assets: Obsolete or damaged inventory must not be carried above recoverable amount.

Lesson 07 · Cash, Receivables & Bank Reconciliation

  • Cash is high-risk: Cash is liquid, easy to conceal and therefore needs strong segregation and authorisation controls.
  • Bank reconciliation separates bank timing from book errors: Reconciling items explain bank balance; adjusting items require journal entries in the books.
  • Receivables are not automatically collectible: Allowance accounting reports receivables at estimated collectible value.

Lesson 08 · Property, Plant & Equipment

  • Capitalising vs expensing changes profit timing: Capital expenditure creates an asset and is expensed over time; revenue expenditure hits profit immediately.
  • Carrying amount is not market value: Cost less accumulated depreciation and impairment is an accounting carrying amount.
  • Disposal combines asset removal and gain/loss: Correct disposal requires removing cost, removing accumulated depreciation, recording consideration and balancing gain/loss.

Lesson 09 · Shareholders’ Equity

  • Equity explains ownership claim: Share capital, reserves and retained earnings show how owners finance and retain returns in the company.
  • Dividends are distributions, not expenses: A declared dividend reduces retained earnings and creates dividends payable; it is not operating cost.
  • Investor ratios depend on equity data: EPS, dividend yield, payout ratio and book value per share interpret profit and equity for shareholders.

Lesson 10 · Current & Non-Current Liabilities

  • Classification affects liquidity and risk: Current vs non-current presentation helps users judge short-term pressure and long-term financing.
  • Tax, GST and interest require separate ledgers: Expense, payable, input tax, output tax and cash settlement are related but not the same account.
  • Debt ratios explain statement risk: Gearing, debt ratio and interest cover use liabilities and profit to interpret solvency risk.

Formula bank for print — formula + where numbers come from

Formulas are deliberately high-contrast. The “where to get the numbers” line tells learners which statement or record supplies the figure.

L04 / L06

Gross profit

FormulaSales revenue − Cost of sales (COGS)
Trace statement inputs:

Sales revenue − cost of sales (COGS) = gross profit.

How to read it:

Use after adjusting purchases, returns, freight-in, closing inventory, shortage/surplus and obsolescence.

L04 / L06 / L10

Gross profit margin

FormulaGross profit ÷ Sales revenue × 100
Trace statement inputs:

Gross profit ÷ sales revenue. Gross profit is sales revenue less cost of sales.

How to read it:

Shows how much sales remains after inventory cost. Do not deduct admin/selling/finance costs here.

L10

Operating profit

FormulaGross profit + Other income − Selling expenses − Administrative expenses
Trace statement inputs:

Gross profit + other income − selling/distribution expenses − administrative expenses / G&A = operating profit / EBIT area.

How to read it:

Profit generated from operations before finance costs and tax in common layouts.

L10

Operating profit margin

FormulaOperating profit ÷ Sales revenue × 100
Trace statement inputs:

Operating profit ÷ sales revenue.

How to read it:

Measures operating return on sales after operating expenses.

L09 / L10

ROCE — Return on capital employed

FormulaOperating profit ÷ Long-term capital employed × 100
Trace statement inputs:

Operating profit ÷ capital employed. Capital employed is normally equity plus non-current liabilities.

How to read it:

Shows return generated on long-term funds. It combines profitability and efficiency.

L10

Sales revenue to capital employed

FormulaSales revenue ÷ Capital employed
Trace statement inputs:

Sales revenue ÷ capital employed. Capital employed normally comes from equity plus non-current liabilities.

How to read it:

Explains how efficiently long-term funds generate sales.

L10

Current ratio

FormulaCurrent assets ÷ Current liabilities
Trace statement inputs:

Current assets ÷ current liabilities.

How to read it:

Tests short-term liquidity. A high ratio is not automatically good if assets are idle.

L07 / L10

Acid-test / Quick ratio

Formula(Current assets − Inventory) ÷ Current liabilities
Trace statement inputs:

Quick assets ÷ current liabilities. Quick assets exclude inventory.

How to read it:

Stricter liquidity test because inventory may not turn quickly into cash.

L07

Receivables turnover

FormulaCredit sales / Average trade receivables
Trace source:

Use credit sales from sales records / SPL sales if all sales are credit, and average trade receivables from SFP trade receivables.

How to read it:

Measures how often receivables are converted into cash during the year.

L07

Receivables collection period

FormulaAverage trade receivables ÷ Credit sales × 365
Trace source:

Use average trade receivables from SFP trade receivables and credit sales from sales records / SPL sales if all sales are credit.

How to read it:

Measures average days customers take to pay.

L06

Inventory turnover

FormulaCost of goods sold ÷ Average inventory
Trace statement inputs:

Use cost of sales / COGS and average inventory. Average inventory normally uses opening and closing inventory.

How to read it:

Measures how many times average inventory is sold in the year.

L06

Inventory turnover period

FormulaAverage inventory ÷ Cost of sales × 365
Trace statement inputs:

Use cost of sales / COGS and average inventory. Average inventory normally uses opening and closing inventory.

How to read it:

Measures average days inventory is held before sale.

L07 / L10

Payables settlement period

FormulaAverage trade payables ÷ Credit purchases × 365
Trace source:

Use average trade payables from the SFP and credit purchases from purchase records. If credit purchases are not given, use the amount specified by the question.

How to read it:

Measures average days taken to pay suppliers.

L07 / L10

Operating cash cycle

FormulaInventory days + Receivables days − Payables days
Trace source:

This combines three separate working-capital timing measures: inventory days + receivable days − payable days. The components are built from SPL/SFP inputs and credit records.

How to read it:

Shows how long cash is tied up in inventory and receivables before supplier credit offsets it.

L10

Gearing ratio

FormulaDebt ÷ (Share capital + Reserves + Debt) × 100
Trace statement inputs:

Debt / borrowings ÷ (share capital + reserves/retained earnings + debt).

How to read it:

Measures how much long-term capital comes from lenders rather than owners.

L10

Interest cover

FormulaOperating profit ÷ Interest expense
Trace statement inputs:

Operating profit ÷ interest expense / finance cost.

How to read it:

Shows how many times current operating profit covers interest cost.

L09

EPS — Earnings per share

Formula(Profit after tax − Preference dividends) ÷ Weighted-average ordinary shares
Trace source:

Profit after tax minus preference dividends from equity/dividend records, divided by weighted-average ordinary shares from the share records. Market price is not used in EPS.

How to read it:

Shows profit attributable to each ordinary share.

L09

Dividend payout ratio

FormulaOrdinary dividends ÷ Earnings available to ordinary shareholders × 100
Trace source:

Ordinary dividends from dividend/equity records ÷ earnings available to ordinary shareholders from SPL profit after tax adjusted for preference dividends.

How to read it:

Shows how much ordinary shareholder profit is distributed.

L09

Dividend yield

FormulaDividend per share ÷ Market price per share × 100
Trace source:

Dividend per share comes from dividend records; market price per share is outside the financial statements. Do not look for market price inside the SPL or SFP.

How to read it:

Investor cash return based on current share price.

L09

P/E ratio — Price/earnings ratio

FormulaMarket price per share ÷ EPS
Trace source:

Market price per share is outside the financial statements. EPS is calculated from profit after tax, preference dividends and ordinary shares.

How to read it:

Shows how many times annual earnings investors are paying for one share.

L09

Book value per share

FormulaOrdinary shareholders’ equity ÷ Ordinary shares outstanding
Trace statement inputs:

Ordinary shareholders’ equity ÷ ordinary shares outstanding.

How to read it:

Accounting net asset backing per ordinary share; not market value.

Additional insights

Free cash flow

FormulaCash flow from operating activities − Capital expenditure
Trace source:

Use operating cash flow from the Statement of Cash Flows and capital expenditure from investing activities / PPE additions. This is a cash-flow interpretation measure, not an SPL/SFP-only figure.

How to read it:

Cash remaining after maintaining or expanding asset base; interpretive, not a journal entry.

Statement analysis reminders

Profitability

Gross margin diagnoses inventory/trading performance. Operating margin diagnoses operating cost control.

Liquidity

Current ratio includes inventory. Acid-test removes inventory for a stricter cash-near test.

Working capital

Operating cash cycle = inventory days + receivable days − payable days.

Solvency

Gearing and interest cover explain borrowing risk and ability to pay finance costs.

Investor returns

EPS, dividend yield, payout and P/E interpret shareholder return and market expectations.
