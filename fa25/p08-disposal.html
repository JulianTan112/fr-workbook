Explanation for disposal gain or loss answer

Yes, $250 is correctly a gain, not a loss. This question belongs under Property, Plant & Equipment because the asset being sold is equipment, which is a tangible non-current asset used in the business.

PPE disposal logic

This is not an ordinary sales-revenue transaction. It is a PPE disposal: remove the equipment cost, remove the related accumulated depreciation, compare sale proceeds with the asset’s carrying amount or net book value, then recognise the difference as a gain or loss.

Calculation bridge

Equipment cost                         $6,000
Less: Accumulated depreciation         (4,750)
Carrying amount or book value           $1,250

Sale proceeds received                 $1,500
Less: Carrying amount                  (1,250)
Gain on disposal                       $  250

The business sold an asset with a remaining carrying amount of $1,250 for $1,500. Because proceeds are $250 higher than carrying amount, the difference is a gain.

Why it is not a loss

Loss condition:
Sale proceeds < Carrying amount

Example only:
Sale proceeds                          $1,000
Carrying amount                        (1,250)
Loss on disposal                       $  250

Actual case:
$1,500 > $1,250, so the result is a gain.

Journal entry logic

Dr Cash                                1,500
Dr Accumulated Depreciation            4,750
    Cr Equipment                               6,000
    Cr Gain on Disposal                          250

Dr Cash $1,500: cash received from selling the PPE. Dr Accumulated Depreciation $4,750: accumulated depreciation has a credit balance, so debit it to remove it. Cr Equipment $6,000: remove the original PPE cost. Cr Gain on Disposal $250: the credits need to balance the debits, and the balancing figure is the economic gain because proceeds exceeded carrying amount.

Statement effect

SFPRemove equipment carrying amount $1,250; add cash $1,500; net asset increase $250.
SPLReport gain on disposal of PPE $250 as other income/gain, not as ordinary sales revenue.
Statement of Cash FlowsReport $1,500 proceeds as an investing cash inflow. Under the indirect method, deduct the $250 gain from operating cash flow because the cash proceeds are reported in investing activities.

Quick check

Cash + Accumulated depreciation − Equipment cost
= $1,500 + $4,750 − $6,000
= $250 gain

Final rule: compare proceeds against carrying amount, not against original equipment cost alone. For cash-flow classification, use the PPE disposal cash-flow bridge: gain or loss affects SPL and indirect operating adjustments; sale proceeds go to investing activities.
