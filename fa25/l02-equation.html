Why It Matters — learning outcome bridge

These points explain why the learning outcomes are not isolated textbook headings. They show what the learner should be able to do in a practice or exam setting.

The equation is the skeleton of every answer

Assets = liabilities + equity explains why every transaction must keep the records balanced.

Statements are connected, not separate islands

Profit flows into equity; equity and balances appear in the SFP; cash movements explain liquidity.

Business structure changes presentation

Sole proprietorship, partnership and company forms affect capital, drawings or dividends and equity labels.

Additional Insights — source-reinforced

Reinforced from the Financial Accounting and Financial Management reference attachments. This is not extra memorisation; it shows how the lesson connects to decision-usefulness, statement analysis and exam interpretation.

Two simple equations

SFP equation: Assets = Liabilities + Equity. Performance equation: Profit = Income − Expenses.

Statement relationship

SPL measures performance; SCE explains owner or equity movements; SFP reports position at date; cash-flow thinking explains cash generation.

Entity assumption

Owner and business are accounted for separately; owner drawings are not expenses.

Applied explanation bridge — business activity to statements

The business-activity model is not a paragraph to memorise. It is the route from real-world activity to the accounting equation and financial statements.

Business activityClean workbook meaningAccounts usually affectedStatement location
FinancingThe business obtains funds from owners or lenders.Share capital, owner capital, bank loan, bonds payable, accounts payable where credit is used.SFP: liabilities or equity; SCF: financing where it is owner or lender funding.
InvestingThe business uses funds to acquire resources that help generate future benefits.Cash, PPE, equipment, land, long-term investments, sometimes inventory as an operating asset.SFP: assets; SCF: investing for long-term asset cash flows.
OperatingThe business uses resources to earn revenue and incur expenses.Revenue, receivables, inventory, cost of goods sold, wages, rent, utilities, payables.SPL: income and expenses; SFP: related working-capital balances; SCF: operating.
Equation bridge: Financing creates claims on assets; investing changes the asset base; operating creates profit or loss, which then changes equity. That is why every transaction must still satisfy Assets = Liabilities + Equity.

Formula focus — accounting equation

L02

Accounting equation

FormulaAssets = Liabilities + Equity
Trace source:

Use the Statement of Financial Position map: Assets = Liabilities + Equity.

How to read it:

Every transaction must keep the equation balanced.

L02

Ending capital or equity for sole proprietor

FormulaEnding capital = Opening capital + owner investment + profit − drawings
Trace source:

Opening capital, new investment and drawings come from the owner capital records. Profit comes from the SPL profit line. Ending capital appears inside equity in the SFP.

How to read it:

Drawings reduce owner claim but are not business expenses.

1. Role of accounting in business

Accounting identifies, records and communicates an organisation’s business activities. It provides financial data through financial statements; finance uses financial capital information for decision-making; economics provides broader context about economic environments.

Types of businesses
Business typeWhat it doesAccounting focus
Service businessProvides services for fees.Service revenue, receivables, expenses.
Trading businessBuys finished goods and sells them to customers.Inventory, sales, Cost of goods sold, gross profit.
Manufacturing businessConverts raw materials into finished goods.Raw materials, work-in-progress, finished goods, production cost.

5. Accounting principles and assumptions

Principles and assumptions
Principle or assumptionMeaningExam phrase
Business entity assumptionThe business is accounted for separately from the owner(s).Owner drawings are not business expenses.
Going concern assumptionFinancial statements assume the business will continue operating in the foreseeable future.Assets are not automatically measured at liquidation value.
Cost principle or historical costInformation is based on actual costs incurred in transactions.Record equipment at cost paid, not estimated resale value.
Accrual concept or revenue recognitionRevenue is recorded when goods or services are delivered, regardless of cash receipt.Service on credit creates Accounts Receivable and Revenue.
Matching principleExpenses incurred to earn revenue are matched against that revenue in the same period.Wages, Cost of goods sold and depreciation are recorded in the correct period.
Accounting period principleBusiness activity is divided into reporting periods.Monthly or annual financial statements require cut-off.

6. Accounting equation and elements

The accounting equation is the spine of the statement of financial position:

Assets = Liabilities + Equity. Equity increases from capital contributions and profit; equity decreases from drawings or withdrawals and losses.
Accounting elements
ElementDefinition or roleExamples
AssetsResources controlled by the business with future economic benefits.Cash, accounts receivable, supplies, inventory, equipment, land.
LiabilitiesPresent obligations to transfer economic resources because of past events.Accounts payable, bank loan, salaries payable, tax payable.
EquityResidual interest after deducting liabilities from assets.Capital, retained earnings, share capital.
RevenueIncome from ordinary activities; increases equity through profit.Service revenue, sales revenue, fees earned.
ExpensesCosts incurred to earn revenue; reduce equity through profit or loss.Wages, rent, utilities, Cost of goods sold.
DrawingsOwner withdrawals; reduce equity but are not expenses.Cash or supplies taken for personal use.

7. Transaction-effect table format

Source facts before using the transaction-effect table:
Transaction fact givenWhat the learner must identify before filling the table
Owner invests $900 cashAsset increases by cash; owner equity increases by capital contribution.
Receives $700 cash for servicesCash revenue earned; profit and equity increase.
Pays $500 wagesCash paid for an expense; profit and equity decrease.
Buys equipment $100 on creditEquipment asset increases and supplier liability increases.
Purchases supplies $200 on creditSupplies asset increases and payable increases.
Buys equipment $300 cashOne asset increases and another asset decreases; no profit effect.
Pays $200 to supplierCash and payable both decrease.
Provides $400 services on creditAccounts receivable and service revenue increase.
Owner withdraws $50 cashCash and owner equity decrease; drawings are not expenses.
Collects $400 from credit customerCash increases and accounts receivable decreases; no new revenue.
Control check: every row must still satisfy Assets = Liabilities + Equity. Revenue and expenses affect equity through profit; drawings affect equity directly.
