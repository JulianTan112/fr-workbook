Petty cash reimbursement system

Upon purchase of items for business’ use, the employee sends the receipt to the petty cash clerk to claim money back. After the petty cash clerk checks that it is an original receipt, the employee is reimbursed based on the receipt amount and the amount is recorded in the petty cash book. The only evidence of the petty cash claim is the accounting record in the petty cash book because the original receipt is returned to the employee after reimbursement.

Source-trace note

The page now shows the prior reconciliation, October cash book, October bank statement and petty-cash-control facts separately. Part (a) uses the bank and book records; part (b) journals only the book-side adjustments; part (c) uses the petty cash paragraph.

Required

  1. Prepare a Bank Reconciliation Statement for Sandoz Singapore as at 31 October 2023. If there are any errors, assume that the bank’s records are correct.
  2. Prepare the necessary adjusting general journal entries arising from (a) above. Narrations are not required.
  3. Identify and explain 2 internal control weaknesses with regards to the petty cash claim system. Recommend changes to rectify the internal control weaknesses of Sandoz Singapore’s petty cash reimbursement system.

Why deposits-in-transit and unpresented cheques stay on the bank side: the business has already recorded them in the Cash at Bank ledger. The bank has not processed them yet, so no general journal entry is passed.
Why EFT, dishonoured cheque, bank charges and book error go to the books side: these items are missing from or wrong in the business ledger. They must be journalised before the ledger balance can agree with the adjusted bank balance.

Why unpresented cheques are deducted from the bank statement balance

Core logic: an unpresented cheque has already been recorded by the business as a cash payment, so the company ledger already treats the money as gone. The bank statement is temporarily too high because the payee has not deposited or cashed the cheque yet.

PointMeaning
Timing differenceThe business has written and recorded the cheque, so Cash at Bank in the books has already decreased.
Bank delayThe bank does not know about the cheque until the payee presents it for payment.
Reconciliation aimDeduct the unpresented cheque from the bank statement balance so the bank side matches the realistic, up-to-date cash balance.
Simple example:
Bank statement balance before presentation        $1,000
Less: cheque written but not yet presented          (200)
Adjusted bank-side cash balance                     $800

Books already show: $1,000 − $200 = $800

Sandoz application: cheque 436 ($1,012) and cheque 455 ($5,300) are deducted from the bank side because Sandoz has already recorded these payments, but the bank has not yet processed them. The deduction prevents cash from being overstated.

Bank statement balance                            $10,109
Add: 31 Oct cash sales not yet banked               4,339
Subtotal                                           14,448
Less: unpresented cheques ($1,012 + $5,300)        (6,312)
Adjusted cash balance                              $8,136

If you do not deduct them: available cash is overstated, future payments may bounce, and the cash balance in the financial statements will be inaccurate.

Explanation for Sandoz reconciliation, journals and petty cash control

Part (a) bank reconciliation bridge

Bank side: start with the 31 October bank statement balance of $10,109. Add the 31 October cash sales deposit of $4,339 because it appears in the cash book but not yet in the bank statement. Deduct cheque 436 of $1,012 because it remained outstanding from the prior reconciliation. Deduct cheque 455 of $5,300 because it appears in the cash book but has not cleared the bank.

$10,109 + $4,339 − $1,012 − $5,300 = $8,136

Cash book side: start with the cash book balance of $9,641. Add the EFT receipt from Core Life of $850 because it appears in the bank statement but not the cash book. Deduct the dishonoured cheque from Kool Work of $2,220 because the earlier receipt must be reversed. Deduct bank charges of $35. Deduct the Ole receipt error of $100 because the cash book recorded $5,566 while the bank shows $5,466 and the source says bank records are correct.

$9,641 + $850 − $2,220 − $35 − $100 = $8,136

Cheque 440 and the $5,529 deposit are not adjusted again because they were prior-month reconciling items that cleared in October.

Part (b) journal-entry bridge
EFT receipt:             Dr Cash 850 / Cr Accounts Receivable 850
Dishonoured cheque:      Dr Accounts Receivable 2,220 / Cr Cash 2,220
Bank charges:            Dr Bank charges 35 / Cr Cash 35
Ole receipt error:       Dr Accounts Receivable 100 / Cr Cash 100

Cash ledger check: +$850 − $2,220 − $35 − $100 = −$1,505; therefore $9,641 − $1,505 = $8,136.

Part (c) internal-control bridge

Weakness 1: the original receipt is returned to the employee after reimbursement. Risk: the same receipt can be reused for another claim. Control: retain the original receipt, attach it to a petty cash voucher and stamp it “PAID”.

Weakness 2: the petty cash clerk appears to check, reimburse and record the claim without independent approval. Risk: unauthorised or personal expenses can be reimbursed. Control: require approval by an authorised officer and separate custody, approval and recording duties where possible.
