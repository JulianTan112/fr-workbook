1. Liability definition and classification

A liability is a present obligation of the entity to transfer an economic resource as a result of past events. Current liabilities are expected to be settled within the next 12 months; non-current liabilities are not expected to be settled within the next 12 months.

Current vs non-current classification
ItemClassificationReason
Trade payablesCurrentNormally due within operating cycle/12 months.
Tax payableCurrentExpected to be paid after tax finalisation.
Accrued directors’ feesCurrentObligation for services already received.
Bank loan due in 10 monthsCurrentDue within 12 months.
Bank loan due in 3 yearsNon-currentNot due within next 12 months.
Debentures/bonds due beyond 12 monthsNon-currentLong-term financing obligation.
Current portion of long-term borrowingCurrentPortion due within next 12 months.

2A. G&A, SG&A, and Selling / Marketing expenses

Learners often mix these labels because different textbooks and companies group operating expenses differently. Use the label required by the question template. If the statement separates selling and administrative expenses, split them. If it uses SG&A, combine both selling and administrative items.

Operating expense classification guide
LabelFull nameTypical itemsStatement treatment
G&AGeneral and Administrative expensesDirectors’ remuneration, auditors’ remuneration, office salaries, office rental, office utilities, office supplies, administrative depreciation, insurance for office/admin assets.Shown under administrative expenses or included in SG&A if the statement uses combined presentation.
SG&ASelling, General and Administrative expensesSelling expenses plus G&A expenses in one combined operating-expense grouping.Deducted from gross profit to arrive at operating profit/profit before finance costs and tax.
Selling expensesSales / distribution / marketing-related expensesSales commission, advertising, marketing campaigns, delivery or transportation-out, sales staff salaries, store depreciation, shop supplies used by sales team.Deducted as operating expenses; not included in cost of goods sold unless the question specifically says so.
Marketing expensesExpenses to promote products/servicesAdvertising, digital marketing, promotion materials, campaign costs, sales events.Usually part of selling expenses; if the statement heading is SG&A, it falls within SG&A.
Financial expensesBorrowing-related costsInterest expense on bank loans, bonds, debentures, finance costs on redeemable instruments.Shown separately from operating expenses in many course statement formats.
Exam rule: classify by the purpose of the cost. Cost to sell or market goods = selling/marketing. Cost to run the office/company = G&A/admin. Cost to borrow money = financial expense.

3. Income tax

Income tax is tax on company profits. Tax expense is recognised in the year in which profit is earned. Tax payable is estimated at year-end and recorded as a current liability until paid.

3A. Profit measures and tax language — EBIT, operating profit and taxable income

These labels are easy to confuse because they sit close together in the company Statement of Profit or Loss. Treat them as a sequence, not as interchangeable names.

Core sequence: Sales − adjusted COGS = Gross profit → add other income → less selling/admin operating expenses = Operating profit / EBIT area → less financial expenses = Profit before tax → less tax expense = Profit after tax.

Profit and tax terms learners must separate

Gross profit

Meaning
Sales less cost of goods sold after shortage, surplus, obsolescence or write-off adjustments.
Where
Subtotal after COGS in SPL.
Warning
Do not deduct selling, administrative or finance expenses here.

Operating profit

Meaning
Profit from operations after gross profit, other income and operating expenses.
Where
Usually before financial expenses.
Warning
Not the same as profit after tax.

EBIT

Meaning
Earnings Before Interest and Tax; profit before deducting interest/financial expenses and income tax.
Where
Usually bridged from operating profit or calculated as Profit before tax + interest expense.
Warning
Interest and tax are excluded from EBIT.

Profit before tax

Meaning
Profit after financial expenses but before income tax expense.
Where
Immediately before tax expense in SPL.
Warning
If interest expense exists, PBT is normally lower than EBIT.

Taxable income

Meaning
Income amount subject to tax under tax rules.
Where
Tax computation, not always shown in SPL.
Warning
May differ from accounting profit before tax unless the question says to use accounting profit.

Tax expense

Meaning
Income tax charge recognised in SPL, including current-year tax and prior-year adjustment where required.
Where
Deducted after profit before tax.
Warning
Expense affects profit; payable is the liability.

Tax payable

Meaning
Amount owed to the tax authority.
Where
Current liability in SFP until paid.
Warning
Do not put tax payable as an expense line.

Profit after tax

Meaning
Profit remaining after deducting tax expense.
Where
Final profit figure in SPL.
Warning
This feeds retained earnings/equity, subject to dividends.

Calculation bridge

From gross profit to operating profit

Gross profit + Other income − Selling expenses − Administrative expenses = Operating profit / EBIT area

From PBT to EBIT

Profit before tax + Interest expense / financial expenses = EBIT, when finance expense is the only item between EBIT and PBT

From PBT to PAT

Profit before tax − Tax expense = Profit after tax

From taxable income to tax

Taxable income × Tax rate = Current-year tax expense / tax payable estimate
Exam wording cue: when the question gives “taxable income”, calculate tax from that figure. When it gives “profit before tax” and no separate taxable-income adjustment, use the figure directed by the question.

4. Goods and Services Tax (GST)

For GST-registered entities, GST collected from customers is not revenue and recoverable GST paid on purchases is not an expense. Output tax is collected on sales; input tax is paid on purchases. The difference is payable to or receivable from the tax authority.

GST accounts
GST elementNormal account natureMeaning
Output GST / GST payableLiabilityGST collected from customers and owed to tax authority.
Input GST / GST receivableAssetGST paid on purchases recoverable from tax authority.
Net GST payableLiabilityOutput GST exceeds input GST.
Net GST receivableAssetInput GST exceeds output GST.

4A. GST deep dive — disposal of machine for cash

This is the common Slide 34 confusion: it looks as if 9% is being applied twice, but it is not. One line shows the total invoice amount including GST; another line isolates the GST payable/output tax.

Facts: Machine cost $80,000; accumulated depreciation $10,000; selling price excluding GST $75,000; GST rate 9%.
Step-by-step calculation bridge
StepComputationAmount ($)Meaning
1Cost - accumulated depreciation80,000 - 10,000 = 70,000Carrying amount of machine.
2Selling price before GST75,000Proceeds that belong to the company, before output GST.
3Selling price - carrying amount75,000 - 70,000 = 5,000Gain on disposal; credit because it increases profit/equity.
4Output GST75,000 × 9% = 6,750GST collected on behalf of IRAS; credit GST payable.
5Total amount collectible from buyer75,000 + 6,750 = 81,750This is why Cash/Receivables is $75,000 × 109%.

Answer to “why 9% twice?” It is not charged twice. Cash/Receivables uses 109% only to show the invoice total. GST Payable uses 9% to split out the GST portion from that invoice. How to use the SFP template

  1. Classify assets into non-current and current.
  2. Show accumulated depreciation and allowances directly beneath the asset they reduce.
  3. Separate equity from liabilities.
  4. Split liabilities into non-current and current based on settlement timing.
  5. Check: Total Assets = Total Equity + Total Liabilities.

Common placement traps

  • Dividends payable is a current liability, not an expense.
  • Redeemable preference shares can appear under non-current liabilities when redemption obligation exists and is not due within 12 months.
  • Inventory obsolescence allowance reduces inventory; it is not a payable.
  • Accumulated depreciation is a contra-asset; it is not a liability.

Contra accounts and impairment placement in company statements

Company statement layouts use several contra accounts. They are not separate liabilities; they are deductions against related assets.

Inventory

Contra account
Allowance for Inventory Obsolescence.
Placement
Current assets: Inventory less allowance.
Logic
Inventory remains an asset, but the allowance reduces it to the recoverable / NRV amount.

Accounts receivable

Contra account
Allowance for Impairment of A/R.
Placement
Current assets: Accounts receivable less allowance.
Logic
The impairment expense appears in SPL; the allowance appears in SFP as deduction from receivables.

Delivery vehicles / office equipment

Contra account
Accumulated Depreciation.
Placement
Non-current assets: PPE cost less accumulated depreciation.
Logic
Cost stays visible while accumulated depreciation reduces carrying amount.
Placement logic: impairment / depreciation expense appears in the SPL. The allowance or accumulated-depreciation account appears in the SFP as a deduction from the related asset.
