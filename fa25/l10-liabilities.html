📑 Current & Non-Current Liabilities

Liability classification, corporate expenses, income tax, GST, dividends payable, bonds and debentures.

Symbol convention: all division in formulas uses the forward slash /, not the old division glyph, so it is visually distinct from the plus sign +. Example: Gross profit / Sales revenue × 100.

Learning outcomes

  • Define liabilities and classify them as current or non-current.
  • Record directors’ remuneration and auditors’ remuneration.
  • Record current-year income tax and prior-year tax adjustments.
  • Explain GST input tax, output tax and net GST payable or receivable.
  • Recognise dividends payable and corporate liabilities.
  • Understand bonds and debentures as non-current liabilities where applicable.

Why It Matters — learning outcome bridge

These points explain why the learning outcomes are not isolated textbook headings. They show what the learner should be able to do in a practice or exam setting.

Classification affects liquidity and risk

Current vs non-current presentation helps users judge short-term pressure and long-term financing.

Tax, GST and interest require separate ledgers

Expense, payable, input tax, output tax and cash settlement are related but not the same account.

Debt ratios explain statement risk

Gearing, debt ratio and interest cover use liabilities and profit to interpret solvency risk.

Additional Insights — source-reinforced

Reinforced from the Financial Accounting and Financial Management reference attachments. This is not extra memorisation; it shows how the lesson connects to decision-usefulness, statement analysis and exam interpretation.

Tax distinction

Tax expense appears in SPL; tax payable appears in SFP until settled.

GST distinction

Output GST is collected on behalf of IRAS; input GST is recoverable when claimable; revenue or gain excludes GST.

Solvency interpretation

Interest cover = Operating profit / Interest payable. Low cover signals pressure to meet finance costs.

Formula focus — source-based formulas

The formula is separated from the explanation. Copy the formula route first, then substitute numbers from the correct statement.

L04, L06 or L10

Gross profit margin

FormulaGross profit / Sales revenue × 100
Trace statement inputs:

Gross profit divided by sales revenue. Gross profit is sales revenue less cost of sales.

How to read it:

Shows how much sales remains after inventory cost. Do not deduct admin, selling or finance costs here.

L10

Operating profit

FormulaGross profit + Other income − Selling expenses − Administrative expenses
Trace statement inputs:

Gross profit + other income − selling or distribution expenses − administrative expenses or G&A = operating profit or EBIT area.

How to read it:

Profit generated from operations before finance costs and tax in common layouts.

L10

Operating profit margin

FormulaOperating profit / Sales revenue × 100
Trace statement inputs:

Operating profit divided by sales revenue.

How to read it:

Measures operating return on sales after operating expenses.

L09 or L10

ROCE — Return on capital employed

FormulaOperating profit / Long-term capital employed × 100
Trace statement inputs:

Operating profit divided by capital employed. Capital employed is normally equity plus non-current liabilities.

How to read it:

Shows return generated on long-term funds. It combines profitability and efficiency.

L10

Sales revenue to capital employed

FormulaSales revenue / Capital employed
Trace statement inputs:

Sales revenue divided by capital employed. Capital employed normally comes from equity plus non-current liabilities.

How to read it:

Explains how efficiently long-term funds generate sales.

L10

Current ratio

FormulaCurrent assets / Current liabilities
Trace statement inputs:

Current assets divided by current liabilities.

How to read it:

Tests short-term liquidity. A high ratio is not automatically good if assets are idle.

L07 or L10

Acid-test or quick ratio

Formula(Current assets − Inventory) / Current liabilities
Trace statement inputs:

Quick assets divided by current liabilities. Quick assets exclude inventory.

How to read it:

Stricter liquidity test because inventory may not turn quickly into cash.

L07 or L10

Payables settlement period

FormulaAverage trade payables / Credit purchases × 365
Trace source:

Use average trade payables from the SFP and credit purchases from purchase records. If credit purchases are not given, use the amount specified by the question.

How to read it:

Measures average days taken to pay suppliers.

L07 or L10

Operating cash cycle

FormulaInventory days + Receivables days − Payables days
Trace source:

This combines three separate working-capital timing measures: inventory days + receivable days − payable days. The components are built from SPL or SFP inputs and credit records.

How to read it:

Shows how long cash is tied up in inventory and receivables before supplier credit offsets it.

L10

Gearing ratio

FormulaDebt / (Share capital + Reserves + Debt) × 100
Trace statement inputs:

Debt or borrowings divided by (share capital + reserves or retained earnings + debt).

How to read it:

Measures how much long-term capital comes from lenders rather than owners.

L10

Interest cover

FormulaOperating profit / Interest expense
Trace statement inputs:

Operating profit divided by interest expense or finance cost.

How to read it:

Shows how many times current operating profit covers interest cost.

1. Liability definition and classification

A liability is a present obligation of the entity to transfer an economic resource as a result of past events. Current liabilities are expected to be settled within the next 12 months; non-current liabilities are not expected to be settled within the next 12 months.

Current vs non-current classification
ItemClassificationReason
Trade payablesCurrentNormally due within operating cycle or 12 months.
Tax payableCurrentExpected to be paid after tax finalisation.
Accrued directors’ feesCurrentObligation for services already received.
Bank loan due in 10 monthsCurrentDue within 12 months.
Bank loan due in 3 yearsNon-currentNot due within next 12 months.
Debentures or bonds due beyond 12 monthsNon-currentLong-term financing obligation.
Current portion of long-term borrowingCurrentPortion due within next 12 months.

2A. G&A, SG&A, and Selling or Marketing expenses

Learners often mix these labels because different textbooks and companies group operating expenses differently. Use the label required by the question template. If the statement separates selling and administrative expenses, split them. If it uses SG&A, combine both selling and administrative items.

Operating expense classification guide
LabelFull nameTypical itemsStatement treatment
G&AGeneral and Administrative expensesDirectors’ remuneration, auditors’ remuneration, office salaries, office rental, office utilities, office supplies, administrative depreciation, insurance for office or admin assets.Shown under administrative expenses or included in SG&A if the statement uses combined presentation.
SG&ASelling, General and Administrative expensesSelling expenses plus G&A expenses in one combined operating-expense grouping.Deducted from gross profit to arrive at operating profit or profit before finance costs and tax.
Selling expensesSales or distribution or marketing-related expensesSales commission, advertising, marketing campaigns, delivery or transportation-out, sales staff salaries, store depreciation, shop supplies used by sales team.Deducted as operating expenses; not included in cost of goods sold unless the question specifically says so.
Marketing expensesExpenses to promote products or servicesAdvertising, digital marketing, promotion materials, campaign costs, sales events.Usually part of selling expenses; if the statement heading is SG&A, it falls within SG&A.
Financial expensesBorrowing-related costsInterest expense on bank loans, bonds, debentures, finance costs on redeemable instruments.Shown separately from operating expenses in many course statement formats.
Exam rule: classify by the purpose of the cost. Cost to sell or market goods = selling or marketing. Cost to run the office or company = G&A/admin. Cost to borrow money = financial expense.

3. Income tax

Income tax is tax on company profits. Tax expense is recognised in the year in which profit is earned. Tax payable is estimated at year-end and recorded as a current liability until paid.

Source facts before the tax payable entries:
Case factTreatment
Estimated tax for year ended 31 Dec 20X3 is $50,000.Debit Income Tax Expense and credit Tax Payable at year-end.
Actual tax paid on 30 Jun 20X4 is $45,000.Debit Tax Payable and credit Cash at Bank for the amount paid.
Actual tax is $5,000 lower than the prior estimate.Reverse the excess payable by debiting Tax Payable and crediting Income Tax Expense.
Estimated tax for year ended 31 Dec 20X4 is $80,000.Recognise current-year tax expense and payable.
Prior-year overestimate = 50,000 − 45,000 = 5,000
Current-year tax accrual = 80,000

Boundary check: Prior-year adjustment and current-year tax are separate steps. Do not net them unless the question specifically asks for only the final payable balance.

Profit measures and tax language — corrected route

Treat these labels as a sequence. Do not use EBIT, operating profit, profit before tax and taxable income as interchangeable names.

L10

Gross profit

FormulaSales revenue − Cost of sales
Trace statement inputs:

Sales revenue − cost of sales = gross profit.

How to read it:

Profit before selling, administrative, finance and tax expenses.

L10

Operating profit or EBIT area

FormulaGross profit + Other income − Selling expenses − Administrative expenses
Trace statement inputs:

Gross profit + other income − selling or distribution expenses − administrative expenses or G&A = operating profit or EBIT area.

How to read it:

Profit from operations before finance costs and tax in the usual course layout.

L10

Profit before tax

FormulaOperating profit − Financial expenses
Trace statement inputs:

Operating profit − financial expenses or interest expense = profit before tax.

How to read it:

Profit after financing costs, before income tax expense.

L10

Taxable income

FormulaTax payable estimate = Taxable income × Tax rate
Trace source:

Taxable income is from the tax computation, not normally a direct SPL or SFP line. The resulting tax charge appears as tax expense; unpaid tax appears as tax payable.

How to read it:

Taxable income is a tax-computation figure; it may differ from accounting profit before tax.

L10

Profit after tax

FormulaProfit before tax − Tax expense
Trace statement inputs:

Profit before tax − tax expense = profit after tax. Profit after tax then feeds retained earnings.

How to read it:

This is the profit available for equity movement after tax, subject to dividends and reserves.

4. Goods and Services Tax (GST)

For GST-registered entities, GST collected from customers is not revenue and recoverable GST paid on purchases is not an expense. Output tax is collected on sales; input tax is paid on purchases. The difference is payable to or receivable from the tax authority.

4A. GST deep dive — disposal of machine for cash

This is the common Slide 34 confusion: it looks as if 9% is being applied twice, but it is not. One line shows the total invoice amount including GST; another line isolates the GST payable or output tax.

Facts: Machine cost $80,000; accumulated depreciation $10,000; selling price excluding GST $75,000; GST rate 9%.
Step-by-step calculation bridge
StepComputationAmount ($)Meaning
1Cost - accumulated depreciation80,000 - 10,000 = 70,000Carrying amount of machine.
2Selling price before GST75,000Proceeds that belong to the company, before output GST.
3Selling price - carrying amount75,000 - 70,000 = 5,000Gain on disposal; credit because it increases profit or equity.
4Output GST75,000 × 9% = 6,750GST collected on behalf of IRAS; credit GST payable.
5Total amount collectible from buyer75,000 + 6,750 = 81,750This is why Cash or Receivables is $75,000 × 109%.

How to use the SFP template

  1. Classify assets into non-current and current.
  2. Show accumulated depreciation and allowances directly beneath the asset they reduce.
  3. Separate equity from liabilities.
  4. Split liabilities into non-current and current based on settlement timing.
  5. Check: Total Assets = Total Equity + Total Liabilities.

Common placement traps

  • Dividends payable is a current liability, not an expense.
  • Redeemable preference shares can appear under non-current liabilities when redemption obligation exists and is not due within 12 months.
  • Inventory obsolescence allowance reduces inventory; it is not a payable.
  • Accumulated depreciation is a contra-asset; it is not a liability.

Contra accounts and impairment placement in company statements

Company statement layouts use several contra accounts. They are not separate liabilities; they are deductions against related assets.

Inventory

Contra account
Allowance for Inventory Obsolescence.
Placement
Current assets: Inventory less allowance.
Logic
Inventory remains an asset, but the allowance reduces it to the recoverable or NRV amount.

Accounts receivable

Contra account
Allowance for Impairment of Accounts Receivable.
Placement
Current assets: Accounts receivable less allowance.
Logic
The impairment expense appears in SPL; the allowance appears in SFP as deduction from receivables.

Delivery vehicles or office equipment

Contra account
Accumulated Depreciation.
Placement
Non-current assets: PPE cost less accumulated depreciation.
Logic
Cost stays visible while accumulated depreciation reduces carrying amount.
Placement logic: impairment or depreciation expense appears in the SPL. The allowance or accumulated-depreciation account appears in the SFP as a deduction from the related asset.

IFRS 16 lease reinforcement — right-of-use asset and lease liability

A lease can create both a non-current asset and a financial liability. Use the three-step lease filter before journalising: identified asset, substantially all economic benefits, and right to direct use.

Lease questionAccounting answer
Does the contract contain a lease?Check identified asset, economic benefits and right to direct use.
What is recognised at commencement?Right-of-use asset and lease liability.
What happens each period?Depreciate the right-of-use asset; accrue interest on the lease liability; reduce the liability when cash is paid.
Where does it appear?Asset under non-current assets; lease liability split between current and non-current liabilities.
