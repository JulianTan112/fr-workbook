Statement of Cash Flows

Operating, investing and financing activities

This page reinforces the business-activity model and the cash-flow classification logic. Use it with the statement layout reference when a question asks whether a transaction belongs to operating activities, investing activities, financing activities, or non-cash investing and financing activities.

Nature of business activity: financing → investing → operating

A business normally starts by obtaining finance, then uses the finance to acquire assets, then uses those assets to earn revenue and incur expenses. This is the bridge between the SFP, SPL and Statement of Cash Flows.

1. FinancingGet funds from owners or lenders
2. InvestingUse funds to acquire resources
3. OperatingUse resources to earn revenue
Financing activities

Where the money comes from

Financing means raising funds from shareholders or creditors. Share issues create owner funding; bank loans, notes payable and bonds payable create liabilities. These claims appear in the SFP under equity or liabilities, and related cash movements are classified as financing cash flows.

Investing activities

What the money is invested in

Investing means acquiring or disposing of resources expected to provide future economic benefits. Cash, receivables, inventory, PPE and intangible rights are assets, but in cash-flow classification the investing section focuses mainly on long-term assets and investments such as equipment, buildings and investment securities.

Operating activities

How the business earns and spends

Operating means the ordinary revenue-generating cycle: selling goods or services, collecting from customers, paying employees, suppliers, rent, utilities and tax. Revenue is an inflow from selling goods or services; expenses are outflows or using-up of benefits to operate the business.

Statement connection: financing explains the liability and equity claims in the SFP; investing explains the asset base, especially PPE; operating explains the revenue and expense lines in the SPL and the working-capital adjustments in operating cash flow.

Core classification logic

Important: the Statement of Cash Flows classifies cash movements. Profit or loss items such as depreciation and gain on disposal may affect SPL profit, but they are not automatically cash-flow lines.
Operating activities

Core trading or income-generating cash flows

Cash received from customers and cash paid for normal operating expenses. Working-capital movements such as receivables, inventory, payables, prepayments and accruals are handled inside operating cash flow under the indirect method.

Investing activities

Long-term asset and investment cash flows

Cash paid to acquire PPE or long-term investments, and cash received from selling PPE or long-term investments. For PPE disposal, use the cash proceeds, not the accounting gain or loss.

Financing activities

Owner and lender funding cash flows

Cash from issuing shares, cash from borrowings, cash used to repay loan principal or bonds, purchase of treasury shares, and dividends paid in the common workbook treatment unless a question gives a different classification rule.

Non-cash activities

Disclose or adjust, but do not count as cash received or paid

Depreciation, amortisation, impairment, conversion of debt to shares, and assets acquired by issuing shares do not themselves involve cash. Under the indirect method, depreciation is added back and disposal gains or losses are adjusted because profit included non-cash or non-operating effects.

Indirect-method operating cash-flow bridge

The indirect method starts from accrual profit and reverses non-cash items, non-operating gains or losses, and working-capital timing differences.

BridgeNet profit + non-cash expenses − non-cash income ± disposal gain or loss ± working-capital changes = cash flow from operating activities
AdjustmentDirectionWhy
Depreciation or amortisationAdd backIt reduced SPL profit but no cash was paid when depreciation was recorded.
Gain on disposal of PPEDeductIt increased profit, but the actual cash proceeds are reported under investing activities.
Loss on disposal of PPEAdd backIt reduced profit, but the cash effect is the sale proceeds under investing activities.
Increase in receivablesDeductSome revenue has not yet turned into cash.
Decrease in receivablesAddCash collected from customers exceeded current-period credit revenue.
Increase in inventory or prepaymentsDeductCash is tied up in operating assets.
Decrease in inventory or prepaymentsAddLess cash is tied up in operating assets.
Increase in payables or accrualsAddExpenses were recognised but cash payment is delayed.
Decrease in payables or accrualsDeductCash payment exceeded current-period expense recognised.

Quick direction rule

Operating current assets: increase = deduct; decrease = add
Operating current liabilities: increase = add; decrease = deduct
Depreciation: add back
Gain on PPE disposal: deduct
Loss on PPE disposal: add back

PPE disposal: SPL gain or loss versus cash-flow classification

A PPE disposal touches more than one statement. The gain or loss is not the cash-flow proceeds.

Why these classifications work

Classifier logic

Normal trading cash                    → Operating
Long-term asset purchase or sale cash      → Investing
Owner or lender funding cash               → Financing
No cash movement                         → Non-cash disclosure or indirect-method adjustment

PPE trap

For equipment disposal, the gain or loss belongs in SPL, but the cash proceeds belong in investing activities. The indirect method then removes the gain or loss from operating cash flow so the proceeds are not double-counted.
