1. Objective of financial reporting

General purpose financial reporting provides financial information about a reporting entity to support decisions by existing and potential investors, lenders and other creditors. These decisions include buying, selling or holding equity/debt instruments and providing or settling loans and credit.

Users are concerned with expected returns such as dividends, principal, interest payments and market price changes. Their assessment depends on the amount, timing and uncertainty of future net cash inflows, the entity’s resources, claims against the entity, and how effectively management has used those resources.

Users and decision needs
Decision focusInformation requiredReason
InvestorsProfitability, cash generation, financial position, dividends, riskAssess whether to buy, hold or sell shares.
Lenders / creditorsLiquidity, solvency, cash flows, collateral, liabilitiesAssess whether principal and interest can be repaid.
SuppliersPayment history, liquidity, going concernDecide whether to grant credit terms.
Regulators / governmentCompliance, taxable income, accountabilityMonitor reporting quality and taxation/social policy.
ManagementDetailed internal performance dataPlan, control and allocate resources.

2. What financial reports can and cannot do

Limitations of financial reporting
FeatureMeaningExam implication
Decision-support, not valuation reportFinancial statements are not designed to show the market value of the whole entity.Do not say financial statements equal company value.
Incomplete by designGeneral purpose reports cannot provide every item needed by every user.State that users may need economic, industry and non-financial information too.
Estimate and judgement basedDepreciation, impairment, provisions and fair values involve judgement and models.Faithful representation does not mean perfect certainty.
Cost constraint appliesBenefits of information should justify the cost of providing it.Not every possible disclosure is required.

4. Regulatory framework: GAAP, FRS, IFRS and standard-setting

GAAP refers to commonly followed accounting rules and standards in a jurisdiction. FRS provides principles for preparing financial statements and specifies the type and amount of information to be provided. In Singapore, the Accounting Standards Committee prescribes accounting standards for companies and other entities, with the policy intention of aligning with IFRS where appropriate to local circumstances.

Need for standards
Why FRS is neededAccounting effect
StandardisationEntities apply common recognition, measurement and presentation rules.
ComparabilityUsers can compare organisations and periods more meaningfully.
AssuranceAuditors and users gain greater confidence in the basis of preparation.
Completeness and consistencyRelevant information is not omitted or changed arbitrarily.
Resistance to pressureAccountants and auditors can refer to standards when resisting biased reporting demands.

6. Compliance and ethics

Compliance means following accounting standards and legal reporting requirements. Ethics goes further: it requires neutrality, honesty, professional judgement and resistance to manipulation. Biased estimates, selective omission and misleading classification damage the usefulness of financial reports even when a report appears technically organised.

Ethics checklist
Ethical issueReporting riskCorrect response
Management pressure to improve profitOverstated revenue, understated expenses or hidden liabilities.Apply standards; document judgement; do not bias estimates.
Incomplete disclosureUsers misunderstand risk or obligations.Include material information needed for decision-making.
Inconsistent treatmentComparability is weakened.Apply policies consistently or explain justified changes.

7. Threats to Fundamental Principles

A circumstance or relationship may create more than one threat, and one threat may affect more than one fundamental principle. The five threat categories are tested because they turn ethics from a vague “be honest” idea into a classification exercise.

Five threats to fundamental principles
ThreatWhat it meansCommon exam cueLikely principle at risk
Self-interestA financial or other personal interest inappropriately influences professional judgement or behaviour.Bonus/incentive compensation, employment security, personal use of company assets, loan/guarantee from employer, commercial pressure.Objectivity, integrity, professional behaviour.
Self-reviewThe accountant relies on, or fails to properly evaluate, a previous judgement/activity performed by themselves or their firm/employer.Person who prepared or supported a decision later reviews or determines the accounting treatment for that same decision.Objectivity, professional competence and due care.
AdvocacyThe accountant promotes a client’s or employer’s position to the point that objectivity is compromised.Acting like a promoter for management’s position, especially where the statement may mislead users.Objectivity, integrity.
FamiliarityA long or close relationship makes the accountant too sympathetic to another party’s interests or too accepting of their work.Close family member involved in financial reporting, long association with business contacts, gifts or preferential treatment that are not trivial.Objectivity, professional behaviour.
IntimidationThe accountant is deterred from acting objectively because of actual or perceived pressure or undue influence.Threat of dismissal/replacement, dominant personality pressuring the accountant, fear of consequences if the accountant disagrees.Objectivity, integrity, professional behaviour.

How to answer ethics threat questions

  1. State the fundamental principle affected.
  2. Name the threat category.
  3. Explain the fact pattern that creates the threat.
  4. State a safeguard or action: remove/reduce the threat, decline/discontinue the activity, or resign if it cannot be reduced to an acceptable level.

Quick memory handle

S-SAFI: Self-interest, Self-review, Advocacy, Familiarity, Intimidation. In exam answers, do not merely list the five names. Tie the name to the scenario.
