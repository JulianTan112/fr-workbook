📒 Recording Business Transactions

How business transactions move from source documents into the general journal, general ledger, trial balance, and financial statements.

Accounting cycle Source documents General journal AED LRC Double entry General ledger Trial balance Financial statements Practice Why it mattersAdditional insightsApplied bridge

Learning outcomes

  • Explain the accounting cycle from source document to financial statements.
  • Identify the evidence and transaction details supplied by receipts, invoices and cheque vouchers.
  • Prepare general journal entries with date, debit account, credit account, amounts, posting references and narration.
  • Apply AED LRC debit or credit logic and keep the accounting equation in balance.
  • Post journal entries to ledger accounts and prepare a trial balance.
  • Use account balances to prepare the Statement of Profit or Loss, Statement of Changes in Equity and Statement of Financial Position.

What the learner must be able to produce

TaskAccounting output
Analyse a transactionIdentify accounts affected and whether each increases or decreases.
JournaliseGeneral Journal with debit line(s), credit line(s), amounts and narration.
PostGeneral Ledger or T-account showing running account balances.
CheckTrial Balance where total debit balances equal total credit balances.
ReportSPL, SCE and SFP using ledger balances.

Why It Matters — learning outcome bridge

These points explain why the learning outcomes are not isolated textbook headings. They show what the learner should be able to do in a practice or exam setting.

The cycle turns evidence into statements

Source documents are analysed, journalised, posted to ledger, checked by trial balance and summarised in statements.

Debit or credit is a language system

AED or LRC prevents guessing and lets learners build entries from account nature.

Trial balance is only an arithmetic check

It proves total debits equal total credits, not that every treatment is correct.

Additional Insights — source-reinforced

Reinforced from the Financial Accounting and Financial Management reference attachments. This is not extra memorisation; it shows how the lesson connects to decision-usefulness, statement analysis and exam interpretation.

Normal balances

Assets, expenses and drawings normally increase on debit; liabilities, revenue and capital or share capital normally increase on credit.

Posting logic

The journal gives chronology; the ledger gives account-by-account accumulation.

Error awareness

Equal debit and credit totals can still hide omissions, wrong accounts, wrong amounts on both sides or compensating errors.

Formula focus — double-entry control

L03

Trial balance control

FormulaTotal debit balances = Total credit balances
Trace source:

Do not use the SPL or SFP item locator for this. The figures come from the general ledger after posting. The trial balance checks that total debit balances equal total credit balances before statements are prepared.

How to read it:

This checks arithmetic equality, not whether every treatment is conceptually correct.

L03

Expanded equity effect

FormulaAssets = Liabilities + Capital + Revenue − Expenses − Drawings
Trace source:

Do not use the SPL or SFP item locator for this control equation. It is used while analysing transactions: assets, liabilities, capital, revenue, expenses and drawings are built from journals and ledgers before they flow into statements.

How to read it:

Revenue increases equity; expenses and drawings reduce equity through different routes.

1. Accounting cycle: complete route from transaction to statements

The accounting cycle starts when a business transaction or event occurs and ends when the transaction is reflected in the financial statements. It is not just a list of headings. It is the route that determines what format the learner should prepare at each stage.

1. Source DocumentsReceipt, invoice, cheque voucher and other objective evidence.
2. AnalyseUse the accounting equation and account nature to decide debit or credit.
3. General JournalRecord transactions chronologically with narration.
4. General LedgerPost entries into individual account records.
5. Trial BalanceList ledger balances and prove total Dr = total Cr.
6. Financial StatementsPrepare SPL, SCE, SFP and, where required, cash flow statement.
Exam use: If the question says “journalise”, prepare the General Journal. If it says “post”, prepare ledger accounts. If it says “prepare a trial balance”, use ledger balances. If it asks for financial statements, classify balances into profit or loss, equity, assets and liabilities.

2. Source documents: evidence before recording

Source documents identify and describe transactions entering the accounting system. They provide objective evidence that a transaction occurred and supply the details needed for recording.

Source document analysis table
Source documentWhat it provesAccounting details normally extractedTypical account impact
ReceiptCash was received or paid.Date, party, amount, description, whether cash relates to revenue, collection from debtor, payment to supplier or expense.Cash at Bank or Cash account plus revenue, receivable, payable or expense.
InvoiceGoods or services were supplied on credit or purchased on credit.Supplier or customer, invoice date, amount, description, credit terms, GST if relevant.Accounts Receivable or Accounts Payable plus revenue, inventory, supplies or expense.
Cheque voucherA cheque payment was authorised and made.Cheque number, payee, amount, approval, purpose and supporting invoice or receipt.Cash at Bank decreases; payable, expense, asset or drawings may be debited.

Why source documents matter

They are the basis for recording. Without evidence and details, the journal entry becomes guesswork.

What they transmit

They transmit the transaction date, amount, parties involved, nature of the transaction and supporting evidence for audit trail purposes.

3. General Journal: first accounting record

The General Journal is the chronological diary where business transactions are first recorded as journal entries. Each entry increases or decreases the balances of specific accounts.

General Journal anatomy
Part of journal entryPurpose
DateShows when the transaction occurred.
Debit accountShows the account(s) debited; debit line is normally written first.
Credit accountShows the account(s) credited; credit line is usually indented to make the debit or credit structure visible.
Debit or Credit amountsTotal debit must equal total credit for each journal entry.
Posting referenceLinks the journal entry to the ledger account after posting.
NarrationBrief description of the transaction so less detail is needed in the ledger.

Capital credited = cash contributed 15,000 + furniture contributed 1,000 = 16,000

4. AED LRC: normal balance and increase or decrease rule

A E D

Debit nature

Assets, Expenses and Drawings increase on the debit side and decrease on the credit side.

L R C

Credit nature

Liabilities, Revenue and Capital increase on the credit side and decrease on the debit side.

5. Double-entry accounting: rules and worked illustrations

Double entry means every transaction affects at least two accounts, with at least one debit and one credit. Total debits must equal total credits. The accounting equation remains in balance after every transaction.

Double-entry rules
RuleAccounting meaning
At least two accounts are involvedA transaction must show where value came from and where it went.
At least one debit and one creditDebit or credit sides preserve double-entry structure.
Total Dr = Total CrThe journal entry must balance before posting.
Accounting equation remains balancedNet increases or decreases on one side have equal net effects elsewhere.

6. General Ledger: posting into accounts

Recording transactions in the journal is called journalising. Transferring journal entry information to the ledger is called posting. The ledger is a group of individual accounts, and each account records increases and decreases in that specific item.

Source facts used by the cash ledger illustration:

The cash ledger is not a new question. It posts only the cash movements from the Internet Solutions facts above. Non-cash entries such as supplies purchased on credit do not enter the cash ledger until cash is paid.

Cash movementCash effect
Owner deposit on 1 Nov+25,000
Land purchase on 5 Nov-20,000
Cash service revenue on 18 Nov+7,500
Cash expenses paid on 18 Nov-3,650
Payment to creditors on 30 Nov-950
Owner withdrawal on 30 Nov-2,000
Cash ending balance = 25,000 − 20,000 + 7,500 − 3,650 − 950 − 2,000 = 5,900

How to read the ledger

Debit entries increase Cash because Cash is an asset. Credit entries decrease Cash. The running balance shows the amount remaining in the Cash account after each posting.

Why ledger is needed

The journal shows transactions in date order. The ledger groups all movements for one account, so balances can be calculated for the trial balance and financial statements.

7. Trial Balance: list ledger balances and prove equality

A trial balance is a list of all ledger account balances. Its main check is whether total debit balances equal total credit balances.

Preparing a trial balance — three steps
StepWhat to do
1List each account title and its amount from the ledger. If an account has a zero balance, list it with zero in the normal balance column or omit it entirely.
2Compute total debit balances and total credit balances.
3Verify that total debit balances equal total credit balances.
Trial balance classification
Account typeNormal trial-balance columnFinancial statement destination
RevenueCreditStatement of Profit or Loss
ExpensesDebitStatement of Profit or Loss
AssetsDebitStatement of Financial Position
LiabilitiesCreditStatement of Financial Position
Owner’s equity or CapitalCreditStatement of Financial Position or Statement of Changes in Equity
DrawingsDebitStatement of Changes in Equity; reduces ending capital
Important limitation: A trial balance can prove debit-credit equality, but it does not prove every transaction is correct, complete, properly classified or recorded in the right account.

8. Financial Statements: using the records

After ledger balances are checked, balances are grouped into financial statements. The main statements in this lesson are the Statement of Profit or Loss, Statement of Changes in Equity and Statement of Financial Position. The Statement of Cash Flows lists cash inflows and outflows for the period.

Financial statements overview
StatementWhat it reportsPeriod or point in time
Statement of Profit or Loss or Income StatementRevenues earned minus expenses incurred; net profit if revenue exceeds expenses, net loss if expenses exceed revenue.For a period of time, such as a month or year.
Statement of Changes in EquityHow owner’s equity changes from capital, net profit or loss and drawings.For a period of time.
Statement of Financial Position or Balance SheetAssets, liabilities and owner’s equity.As at a specific date.
Statement of Cash FlowsCash inflows and cash outflows.For a period of time.
Statement of Profit or Loss — expense classification
Expense categoryDescriptionExamples
Selling & Distribution ExpensesExpenses incurred directly in selling goods.Sales salaries, showroom, shop or store depreciation, transportation-out or delivery expense, advertising, shop supplies expense.
General & Administrative ExpensesExpenses incurred in administration, office or general business operations.Administrative salaries, office equipment depreciation, supplies expense when not specified, office supplies expense.
Financial ExpensesExpenses resulting from financing activities.Interest expense.
Statement of Financial Position — presentation logic
SectionClassificationMeaning
AssetsNon-current assetsExpected to provide benefits for more than one year, such as office furniture or equipment.
AssetsCurrent assetsExpected to be converted to cash, sold or used up within one year or less, such as cash, receivables and supplies.
Owner’s equityCapital + Net profit − DrawingsShows the owner’s residual interest after considering profit and withdrawals.
LiabilitiesNon-current liabilitiesObligations not due within the year, such as long-term loan.
LiabilitiesCurrent liabilitiesObligations due within one year or less, such as accounts payable.
Presentation format: The account format places assets on the left and liabilities or equity on the right. The report or statement format lists assets, liabilities and equity sequentially and is the format adopted in this module.
